💬 Dialogues
Scene 1 — After the train trip
白
你们上火车后看见王老师了吗？
Nǐmen shàng huǒchē hòu kànjiàn Wáng lǎoshī le ma?
Did you see Ms. Wang after getting on the train?
陈
没有。有些人在看书，有些人睡觉了。
Méiyǒu. Yǒuxiē rén zài kànshū, yǒuxiē rén shuìjiào le.
No. Some people were reading, some people fell asleep.
白
你做什么了？
Nǐ zuò shénme le?
What did you do?
陈
我看了一个电影。
Wǒ kàn le yí gè diànyǐng.
I watched a movie.
Scene 2 — In the classroom, Chinese lesson
王
你们会说汉语了，也会写汉字吗？
Nǐmen huì shuō Hànyǔ le, yě huì xiě Hànzì ma?
You can speak Chinese now — can you also write characters?
生
老师，我听不见。
Lǎoshī, wǒ tīng bú jiàn.
Teacher, I can't hear you.
王
不要说话！请听老师的问题。你们都会写哪些汉字？
Búyào shuōhuà! Qǐng tīng lǎoshī de wèntí. Nǐmen dōu huì xiě nǎxiē Hànzì?
Don't talk! Listen to the teacher's question. Which characters can all of you write?
生
我会写这些字了。
Wǒ huì xiě zhèxiē zì le.
I can write these characters now.

📝 Grammar Points

1. Aspect Particle 了 (le) — Completed Actions

了 placed directly after a verb shows the action is completed. In negative sentences, use 没 — and 了 disappears.

Subject + Verb + 了 + Object (positive)  |  Subject + 没 + Verb (negative)
我看了一个电影。
Wǒ kàn le yí gè diànyǐng.
I watched a movie. ✅
我买了苹果。
Wǒ mǎi le píngguǒ.
I bought apples. ✅
我没看电影。
Wǒ méi kàn diànyǐng.
I didn't watch a movie. ❌ (no 了 in negatives)

2. 都 (dōu) — All / Both

都 comes before the verb and refers to all members of the subject. It cannot start a sentence.

Subject + 都 + Verb / Adjective
我们都会说汉语。
Wǒmen dōu huì shuō Hànyǔ.
We all can speak Chinese.
他们都是学生。
Tāmen dōu shì xuésheng.
They are all students.
我们都看了那个电影！
Wǒmen dōu kàn le nàge diànyǐng!
We all watched that movie!

3. Separable Words 离合词 (lí hé cí)

Some Chinese verbs are made of two parts that can split apart to insert other words — especially 了, time words, or objects.

Verb Part 1 + [了 / other] + Verb Part 2
睡觉 → 睡了一觉
shuìjiào → shuì le yì jiào
sleep → slept (a sleep)
上课 → 上了两节课
shàngkè → shàng le liǎng jié kè
attend class → attended two classes
生病 → 我生病了
shēngbìng → wǒ shēngbìng le
be ill → I got sick

🏮 Culture Study — Travel, Language Learning & Chinese Characters
🚄

China's High-Speed Rail Network

China has the world's largest high-speed rail (高铁, gāotiě) network, with trains travelling at up to 350 km/h. Taking the train (坐火车) is extremely common in China for inter-city travel — faster and more affordable than flying for most routes. Millions of passengers travel by rail every day.

🎬

Watching Movies on the Train

On long train journeys, watching movies on phones, tablets or the train's entertainment system is very common. Many Chinese passengers also read (看书), sleep (睡觉), or chat — exactly as described in the lesson dialogue. Short naps on trains are completely normal!

🖊️

汉字 — Chinese Characters

Chinese characters (汉字 Hànzì) are one of the oldest writing systems still in use, with over 3,000 years of history. While thousands exist, knowing about 2,000-3,000 characters is enough for everyday literacy. Learning to write (写) characters is an important part of Mandarin study.

🗣️

汉语 vs 中文 — Two Ways to Say "Chinese"

汉语 (Hànyǔ) and 中文 (Zhōngwén) both mean "Chinese language." 汉语 refers specifically to the spoken Mandarin language, while 中文 refers more broadly to written Chinese. In everyday speech, both are used interchangeably — learners will encounter both terms.

🏫

Classroom Culture in China

In Chinese classrooms, teachers are treated with great respect. "不要说话" (Don't talk!) is a common phrase used to maintain classroom order. Students are expected to listen attentively, and raising one's hand before speaking is standard etiquette in Chinese schools.

🤒

Health and 生病 (Getting Sick)

In China, getting sick (生病) is often treated with traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) alongside Western medicine. 中药 (zhōngyào, herbal medicine) and 西药 (xīyào, Western medicine) are both commonly used. It's also very common to receive advice like 多喝热水 (drink more hot water!) when unwell.
