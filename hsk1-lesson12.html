💬 Dialogues
Scene 1 — Asking teacher a question
白
王老师，我可以再问您一个问题吗？
Wáng lǎoshī, wǒ kěyǐ zài wèn nín yí gè wèntí ma?
Ms. Wang, may I ask you one more question?
王
可以。你有什么问题？
Kěyǐ. Nǐ yǒu shénme wèntí?
Certainly. What's your question?
白
那个小店卖不卖手机？
Nàge xiǎodiàn mài bu mài shǒujī?
Does that small shop sell mobile phones?
王
我不知道。你可以打电话问一下。
Wǒ bù zhīdào. Nǐ kěyǐ dǎ diànhuà wèn yíxià.
I don't know. You can call and ask.
Scene 2 — Ordering breakfast in a café
员

Nǚshì, qǐng zuò! Nín hē diǎn shénme?
Madam, please have a seat! What would you like to drink?
客
我看一下。请给我一杯牛奶。
Wǒ kàn yíxià. Qǐng gěi wǒ yì bēi niúnǎi.

员
您还要什么？
Nín hái yào shénme?
Would you like anything else?
客

Wǒ hái méi chī zǎofàn. Zài lái zhège miànbāo hé jīdàn ba.

Scene 3 — Ordering dumplings and tea in a restaurant
员
先生，请坐！您要什么？
Xiānsheng, qǐng zuò! Nín yào shénme?
Sir, please have a seat! What would you like?
客
我要一斤饺子。
Wǒ yào yì jīn jiǎozi.

员

Yì jīn jiǎozi sìshí gè.
One jin of dumplings is forty pieces.
客
四十个太多了。我要一半吧。请给我一杯茶吧。
Sìshí gè tài duō le. Wǒ yào yíbàn ba. Qǐng gěi wǒ yì bēi chá ba.
Forty is too many. I'll take half. Please also give me a cup of tea.
📝 Grammar Points

1. Modal Verb 可以 (kěyǐ) — May / Can

可以 expresses permission or possibility. It can also be used as a standalone answer "Yes, you may." Negative: 不可以.

Subject + 可以 + Verb
我可以再问您一个问题吗？
Wǒ kěyǐ zài wèn nín yí gè wèntí ma?
May I ask you one more question?
你可以坐这儿。
Nǐ kěyǐ zuò zhèr.
You may sit here.
可以。/ 不可以。
Kěyǐ. / Bù kěyǐ.
Yes, you may. / No, you may not.

2. Verb + 一下 — Softening Requests

Adding 一下 after a verb softens the action — making it sound brief, casual and polite rather than demanding.

Verb + 一下
你可以打电话问一下。
Nǐ kěyǐ dǎ diànhuà wèn yíxià.
You can call and ask. (brief/casual ask)
我看一下。
Wǒ kàn yíxià.
Let me take a quick look.
请休息一下。
Qǐng xiūxi yíxià.
Please rest for a moment.

3. Double-Object Sentences

Some verbs — especially 给 (give) and 问 (ask) — can take two objects: a person and a thing.

Subject + Verb + Person + Thing
请给我一杯茶。
Qǐng gěi wǒ yì bēi chá.
Please give me a cup of tea.
我给老师一本书。
Wǒ gěi lǎoshī yì běn shū.
I give the teacher a book.
我问老师两个问题。
Wǒ wèn lǎoshī liǎng gè wèntí.
I ask the teacher two questions.

🏮 Culture Study — Tea, Dining & Politeness in China
🍵

Tea Culture in China

Tea (茶, chá) has been part of Chinese culture for thousands of years. China is the birthplace of tea and grows hundreds of varieties — from green tea (绿茶) to oolong and pu-erh. In restaurants, it's common to be served tea immediately upon sitting down, often complimentary.

🥟

Ordering Dumplings by Weight

In traditional Chinese dumpling restaurants, dumplings (饺子) are often sold by the 斤 (jīn, ≈500g) rather than by the piece. A full jin is usually around 40 dumplings — which can be too much for one person, so ordering 一半 (half) is very common.

🙏

Polite Language in Chinese

Chinese uses specific words to show politeness: 请 (please), 可以 (may I), 您 (respectful "you"). In restaurants, addressing customers as 女士 (madam) or 先生 (sir) is standard. This reflects the Confucian value of 礼 (lǐ) — propriety and respectful conduct.

🍳

Breakfast in China

Chinese breakfasts (早饭) vary widely by region — from dim sum and congee in the south, to steamed buns and soy milk in the north. Bread (面包) and eggs (鸡蛋) are a modern, more Western-influenced breakfast option increasingly popular in cities.

👋

一下 — The Art of Softening

The expression 一下 (yíxià) is one of the most versatile tools in Chinese politeness. It turns blunt commands into gentle requests — "看" (look!) sounds abrupt, while "看一下" (take a quick look) sounds warm and casual. Mastering 一下 makes Chinese sound natural and friendly.

🏠

Teacher-Student Relationships

In Chinese culture, teachers (老师) are highly respected — often addressed formally even by adult learners. Asking a teacher a question is considered respectful and shows dedication to learning. The phrase 我可以再问您一个问题吗？ shows both politeness (可以, 您) and eagerness to learn.
