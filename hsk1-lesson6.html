📝 Grammar Points

1. Modal Verb 想 (xiǎng) — Want / Would Like

想 expresses desire, intention or a plan. It comes before the verb.

Subject + 想 + Verb
我想去超市。
Wǒ xiǎng qù chāoshì.
I want to go to the supermarket.
我哥哥不想休息。
Wǒ gēge bù xiǎng xiūxi.
My elder brother doesn't want to rest. (negative: 不想)

2. Serial Verb Sentences (连动句) — Purpose

Two or more verb phrases used together — the first verb shows movement, the second shows the purpose.

Verb1 (go) + Verb2 (purpose)
我想去超市买东西。
Wǒ xiǎng qù chāoshì mǎi dōngxi.
I want to go to the supermarket to buy things.
我们去西安饭店吃晚饭。
Wǒmen qù Xī'ān Fàndiàn chī wǎnfàn.
We go to Xi'an Restaurant to have dinner.

3. Serial Verb Sentences — Manner

The first verb expresses HOW the action is done (manner), the second expresses the destination.

Verb1 (manner) + Verb2 (go)
我们坐出租车去西安饭店。
Wǒmen zuò chūzūchē qù Xī'ān Fàndiàn.
We take a taxi to Xi'an Restaurant.

4. Interrogative Pronoun 怎么 (zěnme) — How

怎么 is placed before a verb to ask about method or manner.

怎么 + Verb?
我们怎么去？
Wǒmen zěnme qù?
How shall we go?
她怎么去超市？
Tā zěnme qù chāoshì?
How does she go to the supermarket?

5. Mobile Number Reading Rule

Phone numbers are read digit by digit, and the number 1 (一) is always pronounced "yāo" instead of "yī" to avoid confusion with 七 (qī, seven).

手机号要一位一位地读
Shǒujī hào yào yí wèi yí wèi de dú
Phone numbers are read digit by digit, with "1" pronounced yāo.

🏮 Culture Study — Phones, Shopping & Dining in China
📱

Mobile-First China

China is one of the most mobile-connected societies in the world. Cash and cards are rarely used in cities — almost everything from groceries to taxis is paid for via mobile apps like WeChat Pay and Alipay, scanning QR codes everywhere.

🔢

Why "1" is Pronounced "yāo" on the Phone

When reading numbers aloud (especially phone numbers), Chinese speakers say "幺" (yāo) instead of "一" (yī) for the digit 1. This avoids confusion with "七" (qī, seven), which can sound similar over a phone line — a clever solution still used today.

🛒

Wet Markets vs Supermarkets

While supermarkets (超市) are common in Chinese cities, many people still prefer "wet markets" for fresh produce, meat and fish — these open-air markets offer fresher ingredients and a chance to bargain, a tradition that has lasted for generations.

🥟

Steamed Buns (包子) Across China

包子 (bāozi) are a beloved breakfast and snack food across China, with countless regional variations — from pork-filled Tianjin baozi to sweet red bean versions in the south. They're often eaten on the go, wrapped in paper.

👨‍👩‍👧

Family Dining Out

Sunday family dinners at a favorite restaurant, as in the dialogue, are a common tradition. Chinese dining is typically communal — dishes are placed in the center of the table and shared, rather than everyone ordering individual plates.

🚕

Taxis and Ride-Hailing

While traditional taxis (出租车) still operate, ride-hailing apps like Didi have become hugely popular in China, similar to Uber. Many young people now book rides entirely through apps rather than hailing taxis on the street.
