рҹ“қ Grammar Points

1. Actions in Progress вҖ” Three Ways

Chinese expresses ongoing actions using еңЁ, жӯЈеңЁ, or е‘ў (or combining them). All three mean "is/are doing..."

еңЁ + Verb  |  жӯЈеңЁ + Verb  |  Verb + е‘ў
жҲ‘еңЁеӯҰд№ гҖӮ
WЗ’ zГ i xuГ©xГӯ.
I'm studying. (еңЁ + Verb)
жҲ‘жӯЈеңЁжүҫе‘ўгҖӮ
WЗ’ zhГЁngzГ i zhЗҺo ne.
I'm looking for it. (жӯЈеңЁ + Verb + е‘ў)
жҲ‘иҜ»еӨ§еӯҰе‘ўгҖӮ
WЗ’ dГә dГ xuГ© ne.
I'm studying at university. (Verb + е‘ў)
жҲ‘жІЎејҖиҪҰгҖӮ
WЗ’ mГ©i kДҒichД“.
I'm not driving. (Negative: жІЎ, not дёҚ)

2. Affirmative-Negative Questions

Repeat the verb (or adjective) with дёҚ in the middle to form a natural yes/no question вҖ” no need for еҗ—.

Verb + дёҚ + Verb  |  Adj + дёҚ + Adj
дҪ еҺ»дёҚеҺ»еӯҰж Ўпјҹ
NЗҗ qГ№ bГә qГ№ xuГ©xiГ o?
Are you going to school or not?
дҪ еҝҷдёҚеҝҷпјҹ
NЗҗ mГЎng bu mГЎng?
Are you busy?
иҝҷд»¶иЎЈжңҚиҙөдёҚиҙөпјҹ
ZhГЁ jiГ n yД«fu guГ¬ bu guГ¬?
Is this clothing expensive?

3. Modal Verb иҰҒ (yГ o) вҖ” Plans & Intentions

иҰҒ before a verb expresses desire, intention or a future plan вҖ” similar to "going to" or "want to" in English.

Subject + иҰҒ + Verb
д»–д»ҠеӨ©иҰҒе’Ңе°ҸжңӢеҸӢзҺ©гҖӮ

рҹҸ® Culture Study вҖ” University Life & Daily Routines in China
рҹҺ“

The Gaokao and University Life

Entering university (еӨ§еӯҰ) in China is a major milestone shaped by the Gaokao (й«ҳиҖғ) вҖ” China's national college entrance exam, taken after 12 years of schooling. The exam is intensely competitive, and getting into a top university is seen as one of the most important achievements in a young person's life.

рҹҸҘ

Studying Medicine in China

Medical degrees (еӯҰеҢ») in China typically take 5 years and are highly prestigious. Chinese medical universities often have large student bodies and demanding schedules вҖ” reflecting exactly what the lesson dialogue describes: "йқһеёёеҝҷпјҢиҜҫеҫҲеӨҡ" (very busy, many classes).

рҹҡҢ

Public Transport in Chinese Cities

Chinese cities have extensive public transport networks вҖ” buses (еқҗиҪҰ), metro systems, and ride-hailing apps. Many university students and young professionals travel by bus or metro rather than driving, making expressions like жҲ‘жІЎејҖиҪҰпјҢеқҗиҪҰе‘ў (I'm not driving, I'm taking transit) very common in daily conversation.

рҹҳҙ

Sleep Culture and Morning Routines

Sleeping in (иҝҳеңЁзқЎи§ү) on weekends is common across all ages in China. University students in particular are known for sleeping late on Saturdays and Sundays after intense study weeks. иө·еәҠ (getting up) is a common topic in family morning conversations!

рҹ‘§

е°ҸжңӢеҸӢ вҖ” Children and Play

The term е°ҸжңӢеҸӢ (xiЗҺopГ©ngyou) literally means "little friend" and is a warm, commonly used word for children or young kids. In Chinese culture, children's play (зҺ©) and social development are valued alongside academic learning, though academic pressure starts early.

рҹҚҪпёҸ

Restaurant Culture in China

Eating out at restaurants (йҘӯеә—) is a major part of Chinese social life вҖ” for family meals, business meetings, and friend gatherings. Finding a good restaurant is a common challenge, especially in busy cities, making the situation in Text 1 (searching for a restaurant while on the phone) very relatable!
