💬 Dialogues
Scene 1 — Talking about students
A
一飞忙吗？她有多少个学生？
Yīfēi máng ma? Tā yǒu duōshao gè xuésheng?
Is Yifei busy? How many students does she have?
B
她有二十个学生。
Tā yǒu èrshí gè xuésheng.
She has twenty students.
Scene 2 — Family members
A
我有两个哥哥，你呢？
Wǒ yǒu liǎng gè gēge, nǐ ne?
I have two elder brothers, what about you?
B
我没有哥哥。
Wǒ méiyǒu gēge.
I don't have an elder brother.
A
你家有几口人？
Nǐ jiā yǒu jǐ kǒu rén?
How many people are in your family?
B
我家有四口人。
Wǒ jiā yǒu sì kǒu rén.
There are four people in my family.
Scene 3 — Children and ages
A
这是您儿子吗？
Zhè shì nín érzi ma?
Is this your son?
B
是的。我有两个孩子，一个儿子，一个女儿。
Shì de. Wǒ yǒu liǎng gè háizi, yí gè érzi, yí gè nǚ'ér.
Yes. I have two children, a son and a daughter.
A
您儿子几岁？
Nín érzi jǐ suì?
How old is your son?
B
他今年五岁。您女儿多大？
Tā jīnnián wǔ suì. Nín nǚ'ér duō dà?
He is five this year. How old is your daughter?
A
她今年十二。
Tā jīnnián shí'èr.
She is twelve this year.
📝 Grammar Points

1. 有 (yǒu) — To Have

Use 有 to express possession. Negative form is 没有 (not 不有).

Subject + 有 + Object
我有一个姐姐。
Wǒ yǒu yí gè jiějie.
I have an elder sister.
我没有哥哥。
Wǒ méiyǒu gēge.
I don't have an elder brother.

2. A……，B呢？ — Asking Back

After answering a question, use 呢 to ask the same question back to someone else.

Statement + ，+ Person + 呢？
我有两个哥哥，你呢？
Wǒ yǒu liǎng gè gēge, nǐ ne?
I have two brothers, what about you?

3. Measure Words & the Special Rule for 2

Numbers need a measure word before the noun. For "two", use 两 (liǎng) before measure words, not 二 (èr).

两 + Measure Word (not 二)
两个人
liǎng gè rén
two people
两个哥哥
liǎng gè gēge
two elder brothers
四口人
sì kǒu rén
four family members (口 used for family)

4. Asking Quantity: 多少 vs 几

多少 (duōshao) is used for any number, especially larger ones. 几 (jǐ) is used for smaller, expected numbers (usually under 10).

她有多少个学生？
Tā yǒu duōshao gè xuésheng?
How many students does she have?
你家有几口人？
Nǐ jiā yǒu jǐ kǒu rén?
How many people are in your family?

5. Asking Age: 几岁 vs 多大

几岁 (jǐ suì) is used for children under 10. 多大 (duō dà) is used for people over 10.

他几岁？
Tā jǐ suì?
How old is he? (young child)
你多大？
Nǐ duō dà?
How old are you? (older person)

🏮 Culture Study — Family in China
👨‍👩‍👧‍👦

The Importance of Family (家)

Family is the cornerstone of Chinese society. The character 家 (jiā) means both "family" and "home" — showing how closely the two concepts are linked. Multiple generations often live together or very close by, and family gatherings are central to Chinese life.

👴

Birth Order Matters

Unlike English, Chinese has separate words for elder and younger siblings: 哥哥 (elder brother), 弟弟 (younger brother), 姐姐 (elder sister), 妹妹 (younger sister). This reflects the cultural importance of birth order and respect for elders within the family.

👶

The One-Child Policy & Modern Families

From 1980 to 2015, China's one-child policy limited most families to one child. Since 2021, families are encouraged to have up to three children. Many young Chinese people today still grew up without siblings, making family vocabulary an important and sometimes emotional topic.

🔢

Lucky and Unlucky Numbers

In Chinese culture, numbers carry symbolic meaning. 八 (eight) sounds like "prosper" and is considered very lucky — that's why the 2008 Beijing Olympics opened on 8/8/08! On the other hand, 四 (four) sounds like "death" and is often avoided, especially in hospitals, hotels and phone numbers.

🎂

Asking About Age

In China, asking someone's age (especially elders) is common and not considered rude, unlike in many Western countries. It often comes from a place of respect — knowing someone's age helps determine the appropriate level of politeness and the correct terms to address them.

🏠

Multi-Generational Households

It's common in China for grandparents to live with or very near their children and grandchildren, often helping to raise the kids. This tradition reflects Confucian values of filial piety (孝, xiào) — respecting and caring for one's parents and elders.
