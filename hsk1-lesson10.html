💬 Dialogues
Scene 1 — Buying a cup in a shop
客
请问，有杯子吗？
Qǐngwèn, yǒu bēizi ma?
Excuse me, do you have cups?
员
有，杯子在这边。多少钱一个？
Yǒu, bēizi zài zhèbiān. Duōshao qián yí gè?
Yes, the cups are over here. How much is one?
员
这些五块钱一个，那些十块钱一个。
Zhèxiē wǔ kuài qián yí gè, nàxiē shí kuài qián yí gè.
These are 5 yuan each, those are 10 yuan each.
客
我买这个吧。
Wǒ mǎi zhège ba.
I'll take this one.
Scene 2 — Buying apples at the fruit market
客
这儿的水果真不少！您想买什么？
Zhèr de shuǐguǒ zhēn bù shǎo! Nín xiǎng mǎi shénme?
There is so much fruit here! What would you like to buy?
客
我想买两斤苹果。
Wǒ xiǎng mǎi liǎng jīn píngguǒ.
I'd like to buy two jin of apples.
员
苹果三块五一斤。这些七块二，七块钱吧。
Píngguǒ sān kuài wǔ yì jīn. Zhèxiē qī kuài èr, qī kuài qián ba.
Apples are 3.5 yuan per jin. These come to 7.2, let's say 7 yuan.
客
好的，这儿的苹果真便宜！
Hǎo de, zhèr de píngguǒ zhēn piányi!
Great! The apples here are really affordable!
Scene 3 — Buying children's clothes
王
这家商店衣服真多！这件一百元，怎么样？
Zhè jiā shāngdiàn yīfu zhēn duō! Zhè jiàn yì bǎi yuán, zěnmeyàng?
So many clothes in this store! This one is 100 yuan, what do you think?
刘
好看，也不贵。小雪能穿，买一件吧。
Hǎokàn, yě bú guì. Xiǎoxuě néng chuān, mǎi yí jiàn ba.
It looks nice and isn't expensive. Xiaoxue can wear it — let's buy one.
王
小明能穿吗？
Xiǎomíng néng chuān ma?
Can Xiaoming wear it too?
刘
不能。这些是女孩子穿的衣服。男孩子的衣服在那儿。
Bù néng. Zhèxiē shì nǚháizi chuān de yīfu. Nánháizi de yīfu zài nàr.
No. These are girls' clothes. The boys' clothes are over there.
📝 Grammar Points

1. Asking Prices — 多少钱？

Use 多少钱 (duōshao qián) to ask "how much?" Place the item before or after.

Item + 多少钱？ / 多少钱 + Measure + Item?
这本书多少钱？
Zhè běn shū duōshao qián?
How much is this book?
苹果多少钱一斤？
Píngguǒ duōshao qián yì jīn?
How much per jin for apples?

2. Chinese Money (RMB) — Spoken Forms

Formal: 元 (yuán) / 角 (jiǎo) / 分 (fēn). Colloquial (more common): 块 (kuài) / 毛 (máo) / 分 (fēn).

三块五
sān kuài wǔ
¥3.50 (short for 三块五毛)
七块二
qī kuài èr
¥7.20 (short for 七块二毛)

3. Adjective-Predicate Sentences

In Chinese, adjectives can be the predicate directly — no need for 是. Add 很/真/不 before the adjective.

Subject + 很/真/不 + Adjective
这儿的苹果真便宜！
Zhèr de píngguǒ zhēn piányi!
The apples here are really affordable!
好看，也不贵。
Hǎokàn, yě bú guì.
Looks nice, and not expensive either.

4. 怎么样 (zěnmeyàng) — Asking for Opinions

Use 怎么样 at the end of a sentence to ask "how is it?" or "what do you think?"

Noun + 怎么样？
这件衣服怎么样？
Zhè jiàn yīfu zěnmeyàng?
What do you think of this clothing?
这个菜怎么样？
Zhège cài zěnmeyàng?
How is this dish?

🏮 Culture Study — Shopping & Money in China
💴

Chinese Currency — 人民币 (Rénmínbì / RMB)

China's currency is the Renminbi (人民币), commonly called 元 (yuán) formally or 块 (kuài) in everyday speech. The symbol is ¥. China uses a decimal system: 1 yuan = 10 jiao (角) = 100 fen (分), though fen are rarely used today due to their tiny value.

🛒

Wet Markets and Fresh Food Shopping

In China, buying fresh fruit by the 斤 (jīn, ≈500g) at open-air markets or wet markets is a daily tradition for many families. Sellers often round prices down slightly as a sign of goodwill — just like in the dialogue where ¥7.20 becomes ¥7.00.

🧧

Bargaining (讨价还价, tǎojiàhángjià)

Bargaining is expected and accepted in traditional markets, street stalls, and some shops in China. However, in modern supermarkets, department stores, and shopping malls, prices are fixed. Knowing when to bargain and when not to is a key part of Chinese shopping culture.

📲

Cashless Payments in China

China is now one of the most cashless societies in the world. Most purchases — from street food to luxury goods — are paid for by scanning QR codes via WeChat Pay or Alipay. Many younger Chinese rarely carry physical 块 (kuài) at all.

👗

Children's Clothing Shopping

Shopping for children's clothes (like in the dialogue) is a common family outing in China. Large clothing markets, such as those in Guangzhou, are famous worldwide for their variety and affordability, supplying clothes internationally as well as domestically.

🍎

Fruit as Gifts and Symbols

Fruit plays a symbolic role in Chinese culture — apples (苹果) represent peace (平, píng sounds similar), while oranges and grapes symbolize wealth and prosperity. Giving a basket of premium fruit is a popular and respectful gift in China.
